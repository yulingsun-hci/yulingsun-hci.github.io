PROJECT OVERVIEW

#HCI #CSCW #Social Computing #Human-Computer Interaction

My research lies in Human-computer/AI Interaction (HCI/HAI), Computer Supported Collaborative Work (CSCW), and Social Computing, with primary focuses on issues of healthcare and aging, education, and rural revitalization. As computing and AI technologies are increasingly woven into the fabric of everyday lives, I am dedicated to deeply analyzing and understanding the needs of different roles in society, as well as the roles, practices, and the impacts of technologies on people's daily work and lives. Drawing on interdisciplinary perspectives from social science, psychology, and computer science, my work aims to inform human-centered intelligent technology design, guiding the development of more accessible, accountable, and equitable intelligent ecosystems, and ultimately fostering more harmonious coexistence between humans and intelligent systems.

GenAI-powered Dementia Intervention

#Dementia #AI #NPI #GenAI

Dementia is a progressive neurological disorder that impairs memory, thinking, behavior, and the ability to perform everyday activities. It affects millions of people worldwide, causing significant challenges not only for those diagnosed but also for their families and caregivers. As the global population ages, the prevalence of dementia continues to rise, making it a major public health concern. While there is currently no effective medical treatment to cure or halt the progression of dementia, Non-Pharmacological Interventions (NPI) have emerged as more effective, safer, and cost-efficient alternatives. In this project, we focus on technology-mediated NPI for people with dementia (PwD), aiming to create more accessible, inclusive, and intelligent intervention solutions, and then to empower PwD, their families, as well as the other stakeholders.

Aging in Digital Era

#Aging #ICT/AI #Retiree

Population aging has become one of the most pressing livelihood and social governance challenges facing contemporary societies. With the rapid advancement of digital technologies, older adults’ lifestyles, forms of social participation, learning practices, and identity construction are undergoing profound transformations. On the one hand, digital technologies offer older adults unprecedented opportunities for social connection, lifelong learning, and self-expression. On the other hand, technological barriers, platform logics, and algorithmic mechanisms may also exacerbate digital inequalities, reshaping older adults’ social positioning and everyday lived experiences. Against this backdrop, this project aims to move beyond singular perspectives focused on “technology usability” or the “digital divide” by grounding its inquiry in older adults’ lived experiences. We examine how older adults learn, collaborate, express themselves, and become embedded in digital society in the digital era. Drawing on HCI and CSCW as its core methodological frameworks, the project seeks to develop a systematic understanding of how digital technologies function as sociotechnical infrastructures that deeply shape older adults’ learning, work, emotional lives, and identity construction, ultimately informing inclusive technology design, public policy, and social innovation in aging societies.

Data/AI-Powered Collaborative Healthcare

#Data/AI #Healthcare #Collaboration #Care Providers

In recent years, growing efforts have been devoted to designing data-driven and AI-enabled systems to connect multiple stakeholders and improve coordination, efficiency, and decision-making in healthcare. However, the practical deployment of these technologies often reveals fundamental tensions, because healthcare, particularly for older adults, is inherently a complex collaborative process involving patients, family caregivers, clinicians, care workers, institutions, and increasingly, data- and AI-driven technologies, deeply situated in specific organizational, cultural, and infrastructural contexts. This project aims to critically examine, and thoughtfully design data- and AI-driven technologies for collaborative healthcare, with a particular focus on investigating how data and AI systems mediate collaboration across multiple stakeholders over time, how they reshape roles, responsibilities, power relations, and care experiences, and how care work is practically accomplished in datafied environments, and then informing the design of responsible AI systems, collaborative healthcare technologies, and policies that are sensitive to the real-world multi-stakeholder collaborative caregiving process.

5. Rural Revitalization in Digital Era

#Rural Revitalization #Infrastructure #Education #Healthcare

Rural revitalization has become a critical social and developmental challenge. Rural contexts are characterized by distinct social structures, infrastructural conditions, cultural practices, and economic constraints, which fundamentally shape how digital technologies are adopted, adapted, and experienced. This project examines the usability, impacts, and unintended consequences of digital technologies in rural settings, with a particular focus on agriculture, education, governance and management, entrepreneurship, and elderly care. We aim to contribute a more nuanced understanding of rural digitalization as an ongoing sociotechnical process, offering empirical insights, conceptual frameworks, and design implications for developing inclusive, context-sensitive digital technologies and policies that genuinely support sustainable rural revitalization in the digital era.

Data-Driven Governance

#Governance #Data-Driven #City Governance #Equitable #Critical Computing

This project examines the use, implementation, and societal implications of data-driven technologies across diverse domains of governance. As data infrastructures, algorithmic systems, and AI technologies increasingly mediate public administration, healthcare, social services, and urban management, governance is being reshaped in ways that extend far beyond efficiency or automation. This project seeks to understand how data-driven governance operates in practice, how it is sustained through everyday work, and what consequences it has for governance processes, social relations, and individual and collective well-being, utimately offering empirical evidence, conceptual frameworks, and design implications for developing more accountable, humane, and socially sustainable approaches to data-driven governance.
